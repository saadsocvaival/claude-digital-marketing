# Narrative Thesis — {Client}

**Framework:** Andy Raskin "strategic narrative" + April Dunford positioning.

## 1. The shift (the "what changed")
A named change in the world — tech, regulation, buyer behavior, economics — that our prospects can already feel. Not "AI is coming"; something specific they're living through.

> {one paragraph}

## 2. The stakes (winners vs losers)
If the shift is real, who wins and who loses? Name both. The loser-narrative is what creates urgency.

> **Winners:** {profile of companies thriving under the new rules}
> **Losers:** {profile of companies that will be obsoleted}

## 3. The promised land
The outcome prospects want — described in their language, not ours. No features. No product name.

> {one paragraph}

## 4. The obstacles (old-world solutions fail)
Why the incumbent solutions can't get them there. Name categories, not competitors.

> {bulleted list of 3–5 obstacles, each 1 sentence}

## 5. Our unique insight
The non-obvious belief that shapes how we build. If a competitor could say it without lying, it's not unique enough.

> {one sentence}

## 6. Evidence of insight
- **Customer proof:** {1–2 quotes that corroborate the insight, not the product}
- **Data proof:** {1–2 stats from research or first-party data}
- **Pattern proof:** {the repeatable mechanism the insight unlocks}

## 7. Call-to-mission (not call-to-action)
What we're inviting prospects to *join*, not just buy. The mission language is what employees will quote back to you.

> {one sentence}

## 8. Usage rules
- Every pitch deck opens with Sections 1–3.
- Every long-form asset (keynote, whitepaper, podcast pitch) begins with the shift, not the product.
- Employees memorize Section 1 and Section 5 verbatim.
- Refresh quarterly if the shift evolves; retire if the shift is no longer felt.
