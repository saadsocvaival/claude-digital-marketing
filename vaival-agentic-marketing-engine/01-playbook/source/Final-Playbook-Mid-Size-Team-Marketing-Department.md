# **EXECUTIVE SUMMARY**

This playbook is the authoritative operational reference for the Digital Marketing Department of a mid-size SaaS company with a team of 15 to 40 people. It is written specifically for C-level executives and senior leadership to provide complete visibility into how the department is structured, what it is accountable for, how each vertical operates, and the standardized procedures, tools, and goals that govern daily work.

At the mid-size stage, the department must punch above its weight. Resources are tighter than an enterprise team, but expectations — from investors, leadership, and the market — are just as demanding. This playbook codifies the operating model that enables a lean, focused team to deliver measurable, scalable results by eliminating ambiguity, concentrating on the highest-ROI activities, and building repeatable systems.

# **2\. VISION, MISSION & PURPOSE**

## **2.1 Departmental Vision**

To be the growth engine that takes the company from its current stage to the next — a lean, data-driven, and creatively excellent digital marketing team that generates qualified demand, builds brand authority, and delivers measurable pipeline contribution with every dollar spent.

| Vision Dimension | What It Means for a Mid-Size Team |
| ----- | ----- |
| Focused Growth | We do fewer things but do them exceptionally well. No channel is maintained 'just because'. Every vertical earns its budget through performance. |
| Brand Authority | We systematically build our position as the most credible and visible voice in our SaaS category through SEO, content, and social. |
| Operational Discipline | Standardized processes and documented workflows allow a small team to produce consistent, high-quality output without constant management oversight. |
| Data-First Decisions | Every significant decision is backed by data. We measure what matters, report it clearly, and use it to improve. |
| Scalable Systems | Every process we build today is designed to scale as the team grows. We build for 2x the team, not just for today. |

## **2.2 Mission Statement**

The Digital Marketing Department exists to fill the company's pipeline with qualified, intent-driven leads from digital channels — while simultaneously building a brand presence that compounds in value over time. We do this by combining SEO, paid media, content, social, email, analytics, and web into a unified, data-driven growth system.

## **2.3 Department Purpose**

The department serves three core business purposes:

* Demand Generation — Attract and capture qualified prospects across digital channels and convert them into sales-ready leads.

* Pipeline Acceleration — Nurture prospects through the funnel faster, support the sales team with content and intelligence, and reduce deal cycle length.

* Brand Compounding — Build a brand presence in search, social, and content that grows in authority and reach without proportional spend increase over time.

# **5\. DEPARTMENT-LEVEL POLICIES**

## **5.1 Data & Privacy Policy**

All marketing activities must comply with applicable data protection regulations. At the mid-size stage, compliance is not handled by a dedicated legal team — the Head of Digital Marketing and Marketing Ops Manager jointly own compliance for all marketing systems.

* Consent Required: No contact may receive marketing emails or be added to CRM workflows without documented opt-in consent.

* Data Minimization: Collect only the PII strictly required for the marketing activity. No unnecessary data fields in lead forms.

* Retention Limits: Contacts with no engagement for 18 months must be reviewed. Inactive contacts without a valid legal retention basis must be purged.

* Third-Party Tools: Any new martech tool that processes contact or behavioral data must be reviewed for GDPR/data processing compliance before use. Document the vendor Data Processing Agreement (DPA).

* Breach Response: Any suspected data breach involving marketing systems must be reported to the Head of Digital Marketing within 2 hours and to the CEO within 24 hours.

## **5.2 Brand & Messaging Policy**

| Policy Area | Standard | Who Enforces |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| Tone of Voice | Confident, human, and insight-led. Reference the Brand Voice Guide. Never jargon-heavy or corporate. | Content Manager on review |
| Visual Identity | Approved colors, typefaces, and logo variants only. Refer to Brand Style Guide. | Designer sign-off on all visuals |
| Competitor Mentions | Factual comparison only. No disparagement. No unsubstantiated claims. | Head of Marketing approval required |
| Claims & Statistics | All data claims must be traceable to a primary source. Cite the source in all content. | Content Editor before publish |

| ----- | ----- | ----- |

## **5.4 Budget Management Policy**

* Annual budgets are set in Q4 and allocated by vertical based on prior performance and strategic priority. The Head of Digital Marketing owns the department budget.

* Each Vertical Lead is accountable for their channel budget and must track spending weekly against the live budget tracker maintained in Notion or Google Sheets.

* Reallocation between verticals exceeding 15% of a vertical's quarterly budget requires Head of Digital Marketing approval and must be logged with rationale.

* All vendor invoices submitted to finance within 5 business days. Unsubmitted invoices are a compliance risk.

* Any unplanned spend above $2,000 requires prior written approval from the Head of Digital Marketing before commitment.

## **5.5 Tooling & Technology Policy**

* All marketing tools must be approved by the Marketing Ops Manager before procurement. No team member may subscribe to a SaaS tool on a company card without approval.

* No unapproved tool may be connected to company CRM, ad platforms, or analytics systems.

* A master tool list with owner, cost, contract dates, and renewal deadlines is maintained by Marketing Ops and reviewed in Q1 each year.

* Tool access is provisioned on a need-to-use basis. Departing employees must be offboarded from all marketing tools within 24 hours of departure.

# **8\. CROSS-VERTICAL PROCESSES & WORKFLOWS**

## **8.1 Campaign Lifecycle**

Every campaign — regardless of which vertical initiates it — follows this standardized 7-stage lifecycle. This prevents campaigns from launching without proper planning, tracking, or post-launch accountability.

| Stage | Required Actions | Owner | Gate to Next Stage |
| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| 1\. Brief | Campaign brief submitted: goal, audience, channels, budget, timeline, success KPIs | Vertical Lead | Brief approved by Head of Marketing |
| 2\. Creative Build | Copy, design, and landing page built to brief | Content \+ Designer \+ Web | Creative QA checklist passed |
| 3\. Tracking Setup | UTMs applied, conversion tracking verified, pixels confirmed | Marketing Ops | Test conversion fired in GTM |
| 4\. Approval | Spend-tier approval obtained (see Policy 5.3) | See Policy 5.3 | Written approval on record |
| 5\. Launch | All channels go live simultaneously. First-hour check done. | Vertical Lead | All tracking confirmed live |
| 6\. Optimize | Weekly performance review. Budget adjusted. Creative refreshed as needed. | Vertical Lead \+ Analytics | Ongoing until campaign end |
| 7\. Review | Post-launch review at Day 7 and Day 30\. Learnings documented. | Vertical Lead | Written review in Notion |

## **8.2 Content Request Workflow**

Content is the shared resource that powers all other verticals. This intake and production process ensures requests are properly briefed, prioritized, and delivered on time.

1. Requestor submits a Content Request in the project management tool using the standard brief template: channel, format, goal, target audience, CTA, SEO keyword (if applicable), deadline.

2. Content Manager triages within 1 business day: priority assigned (P1 \= this week / P2 \= this sprint / P3 \= backlog), writer assigned, deadline confirmed.

3. For SEO content: SEO Manager reviews keyword angle and confirms search intent match before writing begins.

4. Writer produces first draft by agreed deadline. All claims cited. CTA clear. Minimum 2 internal link suggestions included.

5. Content Editor reviews: clarity, accuracy, brand voice, grammar. Returns to writer if significant revisions needed.

6. Vertical Lead gives final approval. For Tier 2/3 content: Head of Marketing approval required.

7. Designer adds visual assets. Web team uploads to CMS (if applicable).

8. Published with UTM tracking (if distributed via email or paid). Promoted across channels per the distribution plan.

9. Performance tracked at 30 and 90 days. Refresh flagged if traffic below threshold.

## **9.1 Vertical Purpose**

The SEO, AEO, and GEO vertical owns the company's discoverability across every surface a SaaS buyer uses to research solutions — traditional search engines, AI-generated answer boxes, and large language model tools like ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, and Gemini. At the mid-size stage, this vertical must be focused and strategic: prioritize the highest-impact activities across all three disciplines with a lean team, and resist the urge to chase every new tactic before the foundations are solid.

| The Three Disciplines — Mid-Size Plain English SEO (Search Engine Optimization): Getting pages to rank in Google and Bing. Keywords, backlinks, technical health. The foundation — still the largest direct traffic driver and non-negotiable. AEO (Answer Engine Optimization): Getting our content selected as the answer in Google AI Overviews, featured snippets, and People Also Ask boxes. A buyer who gets our answer directly in Google never needs to click — but they do see our brand as the authority. GEO (Generative Engine Optimization): Getting our brand accurately and favorably mentioned when SaaS buyers ask ChatGPT, Claude, Perplexity, or Gemini 'What tool should I use for X?' — a fast-growing buyer behavior that sends zero referral traffic but directly influences purchase decisions. |
| :---- |

| Mid-Size Priority Order Do not try to master all three simultaneously with a lean team. Execute in this order: 1\. SEO foundation first: Technical health, keyword rankings, link building, content clusters. Without this, AEO and GEO have nothing to build on. 2\. AEO second: Once top-20 keyword cluster content exists, layer in answer optimization — schema markup, direct-answer formatting, PAA targeting. 3\. GEO third: Once brand authority and third-party presence are established, begin structured GEO monitoring and optimization. GEO without brand authority is premature. |
| :---- |

## **9.2 Why This Matters — Executive Context**

| Search Behavior Shift | Commercial Implication for Mid-Size SaaS |
| ----- | ----- |
| Google AI Overviews now appear on 15–25%+ of commercial queries | Organic click-through rates on affected queries have dropped 20–60%. Ranking \#1 no longer guarantees the traffic it once did. AEO determines whether our content is the one Google quotes. |
| ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Gemini are used for vendor shortlisting | B2B SaaS buyers prompt AI tools with 'What is the best \[category\] software for a growing team?' Brands absent from AI responses are absent from consideration — without knowing it. |
| 60%+ of Google searches end without a click (zero-click) | Traditional organic sessions undercount our actual search influence. Impression share, PAA presence, and AI mention rate must be tracked alongside sessions. |
| Third-party mentions drive LLM brand perception | What AI tools say about the brand is shaped by G2 reviews, Capterra listings, media coverage, and analyst citations. Managing these sources is now a search function, not just a PR function. |

## **9.5 Core Workflows**

### **Workflow A: Monthly Keyword & Query Research (SEO \+ AEO)**

10. Export current rankings from SEMrush for all tracked keywords.

11. Identify Quick Win keywords: commercial or navigational terms ranking positions 8–20.

12. Run competitor keyword gap analysis: find keywords top 3 competitors rank for that we do not.

13. Categorize by funnel stage: TOFU (awareness), MOFU (consideration), BOFU (decision).

14. Add AEO layer: use AlsoAsked or SEMrush PAA tracker to identify question-format queries related to each keyword cluster. These are AEO targets.

15. Score all opportunities: Search Volume x Commercial Intent x Keyword Difficulty. Prioritize top 15 SEO targets and top 10 AEO question targets.

16. Assign SEO targets to content calendar or on-page optimization queue. Assign AEO targets to FAQ content or schema markup queue.

17. Present combined priorities to Content Manager. Confirm next month's content topics and formats.

### **Workflow B: Technical SEO \+ AEO Audit Cycle**

| Audit | Frequency | Tool | Action |
| ----- | ----- | ----- | ----- |
| Crawl errors \+ index issues | Weekly | Google Search Console | Submit fixes to Web Dev task board within 24 hours |
| Core Web Vitals check | Weekly | GSC \+ PageSpeed Insights | Flag regressions to Web Dev same day |
| AI crawler access review | Monthly | robots.txt \+ llms.txt check | Confirm LLM crawlers allowed on key marketing pages; restrict on sensitive/internal pages |
| Schema markup audit | Monthly | Google Rich Results Test \+ Screaming Frog | Validate FAQ, HowTo, Article, Organization schemas on all eligible pages |
| Full site crawl | Monthly | Screaming Frog | Priority-ranked fix report shared with Web Dev Lead |
| Featured snippet \+ AI Overview audit | Monthly | SEMrush \+ manual SERP checks | List target queries with competitor snippets to displace; check AI Overview citation status |
| Backlink profile check | Monthly | Ahrefs / SEMrush | Identify link wins; flag toxic links for disavow file |
| Full technical SEO audit | Quarterly | Screaming Frog \+ SEMrush | Full roadmap document sent to Web Dev Lead for next sprint |

### **Workflow C: AEO — Answer Engine Optimization (Monthly)**

18. Identify the top 20 question-format queries in our keyword universe using AlsoAsked and SEMrush PAA data.

19. Audit each query in Google: Is there a featured snippet? AI Overview? PAA box? Who owns it — us or a competitor?

20. For competitor-owned snippets: analyze their content structure. What format, length, and directness earned selection? Replicate and improve.

21. Update or create content for each target query: place a concise, direct answer (40–60 words) at the top of the page before any preamble. Structure subheadings as questions.

22. Implement FAQ schema on all pages with Q\&A content. HowTo schema on all process/tutorial pages. Validate in Google Rich Results Test.

23. Track snippet and PAA ownership weekly in SEMrush. Report gains and losses in monthly SEO dashboard.

### **Workflow D: GEO — Generative Engine Optimization (Monthly)**

24. Define and maintain the GEO Prompt Bank: 20 standard prompts representing how a SaaS buyer researches our category. Examples: 'What is the best \[category\] tool for a growing SaaS company?', 'Compare \[us\] vs \[Competitor A\]', 'What are the pros and cons of \[our product\]?'

25. Run the full Prompt Bank across ChatGPT (GPT-4o), Claude, Google Gemini, Perplexity, and Microsoft Copilot. Document every response verbatim in the GEO Response Log (Notion template).

26. Analyze: Is our brand mentioned? What position? Is the description accurate and favorable? Which sources are cited in the responses?

27. Identify GEO Citation Sources from responses: these publications, review platforms, and sites are where LLMs retrieve their answers. Prioritize brand presence on those exact sources (G2, Capterra, TechCrunch, Gartner, relevant SaaS blogs).

28. Ensure G2, Capterra, and Trustpilot profiles are complete, accurate, and actively generating reviews. These are among the most-cited sources in AI vendor comparison responses.

29. For key product and use-case pages: review for LLM readability — clear, factual, direct prose. Remove vague marketing superlatives. LLMs retrieve factual content, not promotional copy.

30. Implement llms.txt file (if not yet live): a structured guide for AI crawlers pointing to the most authoritative company and product content on the site.

31. Produce monthly GEO summary: brand mention rate across 5 AI tools, prompt-by-prompt highlights, citation source status, recommended content or PR actions. Share with Head of Marketing.

### **Workflow E: Link Building & Digital PR (Monthly — Serves SEO \+ GEO)**

32. Set monthly target: minimum 4–6 quality backlinks from SaaS-relevant or industry publications (DR 40+ minimum; DR 60+ prioritized).

33. Build prospect list combining: Ahrefs Link Intersect, competitor backlink gap, AND the GEO Citation Source list (publications AI tools cite in our category).

34. Vet each prospect: category relevance, DR 40+, organic traffic \> 1K/month, spam score \< 20%.

35. Send personalized outreach. Track in spreadsheet or Pitchbox. No mass-blast. One follow-up after 5 business days.

36. Prioritize original research and data collaborations — these generate backlinks AND become AI citation sources simultaneously.

37. Log all acquired links: date, publication, DR, anchor text, target URL, and whether it is a known GEO citation source.

## **9.7 Best Practices**

### **SEO Best Practices**

* Topical authority over isolated pages: Build content clusters around 5–8 core topic pillars. A pillar page supported by 8–12 cluster articles consistently outranks isolated pages on every keyword in the cluster.

* Refresh before creating new: An existing page ranking 8–25 for a target keyword benefits more from a content refresh than a new competing page. Refreshes are faster, cheaper, and compound existing domain authority.

* Internal linking every time: Every new article links out to 3+ relevant existing pages and receives 2+ internal links from existing content. This is non-negotiable and takes 10 minutes per publish.

* Search intent compliance: Match content format to SERP intent. If top-ranking results are comparison listicles, publish a comparison listicle — not a long-form thought leadership piece.

* No cannibalization: Before any new page publish, confirm no existing page targets the same primary keyword. Cannibalization is one of the most common and most damaging mid-size SEO mistakes.

* Patience with reporting: SEO compounds over 6–18 months. Report to leadership on trajectory and keyword movement trends — not just current traffic. One good month does not signal success; one bad month does not signal failure.

### **AEO Best Practices**

* Direct answer first, every time: For question-format content, lead with a concise direct answer in the first 40–60 words. Do not bury the answer after three paragraphs of introduction. Search engines extract answers from the first clear, direct response to the question in the content.

* Format headings as questions: 'What is X?', 'How does Y work?', 'Why does Z matter?' — this structure directly mirrors how AI Overviews and People Also Ask boxes surface information, and signals to Google that this content is an answer, not just an article.

* Schema markup is mandatory for AEO: FAQ schema and HowTo schema increase eligibility for rich results and AI Overview citations. At mid-size, prioritize implementing these on the top 20 content assets first, then expand site-wide.

* Concision beats comprehensiveness for answer selection: Long padded introductions actively reduce the probability of featured snippet selection. Edit aggressively. Give the answer, then give the evidence.

* Monitor AI Overviews actively: Run manual SERP checks weekly on the top 30 commercial queries. When AI Overviews appear and our content is not cited — that is an AEO priority fix, not just a content issue.

### **GEO Best Practices**

* Write for LLM retrieval, not just for humans: LLMs retrieve content that is factually specific, clearly structured, and free of vague marketing language. 'Leading platform', 'best-in-class', and 'revolutionary solution' are ignored. 'Reduces onboarding time by 40% for teams between 50 and 500 people' is retrieved. Specificity is the GEO lever.

* Third-party authority is the GEO currency at mid-size: A 25-person team cannot out-publish every LLM training source. But it can systematically ensure G2, Capterra, relevant SaaS publications, and industry newsletters accurately represent the brand. These sources are heavily weighted by every major AI tool. Treat review generation as a search investment.

* Publish original data: Even one well-promoted original research piece per year — a benchmark report, survey data, or proprietary analysis — generates backlinks AND becomes a GEO citation source. LLMs are programmed to retrieve and cite data from primary sources.

* llms.txt is worth implementing now: It takes less than 2 hours and signals to AI crawlers exactly which pages represent the authoritative voice of the company. As LLM crawling matures, this file will carry increasing weight.

* Do not panic about GEO results in the short term: LLM training and retrieval systems have significant lag. Content and brand presence built today will influence AI responses in weeks to months. The GEO KPIs are trend metrics — measure month-over-month direction, not absolute position.

* Accuracy matters as much as mention: Being mentioned inaccurately in an AI response (wrong pricing, wrong use case, outdated features) can actively harm sales conversations. Monitor response accuracy monthly and address inaccuracies by updating the third-party sources that AI tools cite.

## **10.6 Paid Media Best Practices**

## **15.1 Vertical Purpose**

The Website Development vertical owns the company's most important digital asset — the marketing website. For a SaaS company, the website is simultaneously the primary sales tool, the brand's front door, the SEO platform, and the lead capture engine. At the mid-size stage, this is often a small but disproportionately impactful team. Every conversion rate improvement delivers multiplied returns across all channels that send traffic to the site.

It should never be in a state of 'done'. Every week there should be at least one optimization, test, or improvement in progress. The highest-leverage activities are: improving landing page CVR, improving Core Web Vitals, and building new campaign-specific landing pages faster. |

### **Workflow B: CRO Testing Cycle**

60. CRO Specialist identifies test opportunity from: heatmap data, session recordings, GA4 funnel drop-off analysis, or conversion rate benchmarking.

61. Test hypothesis documented: \[Observation\] → \[Hypothesis\] → \[Predicted outcome\] → \[Metric to measure and target sample size\].

62. Test prioritized using PIE score: Potential impact (1–10) x Importance to business (1–10) x Ease of implementation (1–10). Run highest-scoring tests first.

63. Control and variant designed in Figma by UX Designer. Approved by Web Dev Lead.

64. Test implemented in VWO or equivalent by Front-End Developer.

65. Test runs until 95% statistical significance OR minimum 2 weeks (whichever comes later). Do not end tests early.

66. Results analyzed by CRO Specialist. If winner: implement permanently. If inconclusive: document learnings and re-test with refined hypothesis.

67. Test learnings added to CRO Knowledge Base in Notion. Shared in next weekly marketing sync.

## **15.7 Website Best Practices**

* Mobile-first design: Design every page for mobile first. Majority of SaaS marketing site traffic is mobile. Desktop is secondary.

* Paid landing pages: Remove navigation, reduce exit points. One page, one goal, one CTA. Never send paid traffic to the generic homepage.

* Speed is a commercial metric: A 1-second improvement in page load time can increase conversions by 7%. Treat load speed as a revenue lever, not a technical nicety.

* Design system discipline: Every new page uses components from the established design system. Ad-hoc designs that bypass the system create brand inconsistency and future technical debt.

* Analytics on every page: No page goes live without confirmed GA4 event tracking, GTM tags firing, and conversion goals configured.

* Never assume, always test: Any significant change to high-traffic pages (homepage, pricing, demo request) should be A/B tested. Opinion-based changes are the enemy of conversion optimization.

* Accessibility is business-critical: WCAG 2.1 AA compliance expands the addressable audience and protects the company legally in key markets.
